3-Day Outlook: Saturday - Monday

A Wind Chill Warning is in effect until 1 PM ET this afternoon. A Winter Weather Advisory is in effect until 5 PM ET this afternoon. Dangerously cold wind chills and blowing snow. Wind  chills as low as 45 below zero. Winds gusting as high as 40 mph. Areas of blowing snow could significantly reduce visibility and cause localized hazardous travel. Strong winds could cause tree damage. The dangerously cold wind chills could cause frostbite on exposed skin in as little as 10 minutes.

NWS Outlook: Strong low pressure will track east of Maine today. High pressure will cross the region Sunday. Low pressure will pass just west of the state on Monday and exit across the Maritimes Tuesday. A strong pressure gradient resulting from a departing storm over
the Maritimes and high pressure building in from the Great Lakes will result in very windy conditions today. A few clouds and flurries are possible across the far northern border where a few streamer bands from the open Saint Lawrence River may reach places such as Frenchville.

Winds decrease around and after sunset, ending the blowing snow and wind chill hazards. High pressure builds across the North Woods by daybreak Sunday, which along with clear skies will allow temperatures to drop into the -20s across the colder valleys. Other areas will see lows in the single digits below to teens below zero.

Sunday will be a decent mid-winter day with plenty of sunshine, lighter wind, and high temperatures that will be a good 10 degrees above the highs on Saturday. Attention Sunday night turns to low pressure that will be moving up the Mid-Atlantic coast.  It will remain clear during the evening across the region with clouds increasing overnight.  Lows across the Valley will likely be around or shortly after midnight.

On Monday, most of the models and their respective ensembles take the primary low either along/near the western Maine/Quebec border or up the Saint Lawrence River Valley. This will create a surge of warm air to the north during the day Monday. Precipitation will spread north and should start mid-morning Downeast and reach far northern areas by late morning to early afternoon.

Initially all areas should start as snow, but northeast areas may see a mix or change to rain by Monday evening. At this point, this is looking like an advisory level event with 4 to 6 inches of snow across the eastern and central portions of the Valley with 6-8 inches in the North Woods and Allagash. Significant ice accretion looks unlikely at this time.

Days 4 to 7: Tuesday - Friday

NWS Outlook: Windy and much colder on Tuesday with continued cold air moving into the SJV with temperatures likely falling throughout the day. There could be marginal wind chill concerns across the area.  High pressure builds in Tuesday night, but it may not be fast enough to mitigate some low wind chills during the evening.  The air mass will be quite cold and, if the western valleys do see calm winds and remain clear, could see -30F by daybreak Wednesday.

The next system moves in from the west on Wednesday with increasing clouds and the chance of overrunning snow during the afternoon. At this time it looks like a fast moving low with a couple of inches of snow.  The system pulls away from the area Wednesday night with another shot of colder air to follow for Thursday into Friday with below average temperatures.
