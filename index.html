NWS Caribou Hazardous Weather Outlook
The major warming trend is expected to last through midweek with well above average temperatures. A precipitation event is likely Friday into Saturday. However, this event is trending colder with an increasing possibility of precipitation transitioning to a wintry mix and snow across northern areas later Friday. Colder temperatures would also help limit snow melt across northern areas late week. Ice breakup is expected on southern river basins such as the Penobscot and Piscataquis by midweek. Late week ice breakup across northern rivers is more uncertain, though still possible. The threat of ice jams and localized flooding is still a concern. Please continue to monitor the latest forecasts from the National Weather Service.

Short-Range Outlook: Tuesday - Thursday
High pressure will remain across the Valley today with partly cloudy skies and dry conditions continuing. A weak cold front will approach today; however, it is expected to stall out around the St. Lawrence with only a slight chance for precipitation associated with it.

Mostly cloudy skies develop Wednesday as east/southeast flow draws marine air over the region. Low pressure tracks northwest of the region Wednesday into Thursday drawing an occluded front across the region triggering some shower activity after midnight.

Considerable cloudiness is expected across the SJV Thursday with some lessening in cloud cover in the afternoon as dry air and daytime heating reduce moisture aloft. However, another low pressure system tracking east from the Great Lakes will increase clouds Thursday night along with increasing chances for precipitation by Friday morning.

Medium Range Outlook: Friday - Monday

Friday will be mostly cloudy with rain likely in the afternoon as low pressure advances towards the region. Colder air moves into the Valley Friday night producing a mix of rain, sleet, and snow with a period of snow early Saturday. Snow showers are expected to continue Saturday morning then mix with some rain showers in the afternoon before precipitation ends as the low pressure moves east in the Maritimes.

Partly sunny skies develop Sunday with increasing clouds in the afternoon with rain and snow shower overspreading the area ahead of another low pressure system moving east from the Great Lakes. This low and another over southern New England will draw another occluded front across the Valley Monday with snow expected mixing with rain Monday afternoon. Snow showers linger into the evening hours before ending overnight into Tuesday morning.

NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center Forecast Discussion

Today's Sun
This near real-time image of the earth-facing side of the Sun is close to the Sun's actual appearance. Sunspots appear as dark splotches on the solar disk. They are associated with increased solar activity, including solar flares and coronal mass ejections [CMEs]. CMEs can trigger aurora displays on earth along with other geomagnetic phenomena. The sunspot images update every hour (click for larger image and see timestamp at lower left). Visit NASA's Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO) for more images and information.



 Coronal Holes
The dark areas - called coronal holes - are places where very little radiation is emitted, yet are the main source of solar wind particles. View 48-hour video.


Filaments and prominences

This channel is especially good at showing areas where cooler dense plumes of plasma (filaments and prominences) are located above the visible surface of the Sun. View 48-hour video.

Extreme ultraviolet wavelength displays solar flare activity. View 48-hour video.


Sunspot Regions Chart
The Solar Synoptic Map has been created daily since 1972 by forecasters at the NOAA SWPC. Map phenomena include active regions (including flare and proton event probabilities for each region), coronal holes, neutral lines (boundary between magnetic polarities), plages, filaments and prominences. Click image for more information (on website click "Details" for explanation of chart markings).


LASCO C2
Closer view of the corona (white circle indicates solar disk location). See image caption below for more information.


LASCO C3
LASCO (Large Angle Spectrometric Coronagraph) artificially blocks the solar disk (the shadow crossing from the lower left corner to the center of the image) to monitor the Sun's corona. Coronal streamers, periodic CMEs, bright background stars, planets (e.g., Venus), and occasionally a comet are visible in the image. The white circle in center indicates solar disk location.

Space Weather Overview

WSA-Enlil Solar Wind Prediction
WSA-Enlil is a large-scale, physics-based prediction model of the heliosphere, used by the Space Weather Forecast Office to provide 1-4 day advance warning of solar wind structures and Earth-directed coronal mass ejections (CMEs) that cause geomagnetic storms. The solar wind is a fast-moving stream of charged particles emanating from the Sun and moving outwards towards the Earth and planets. The solar wind contains significant variations in density (top graph) and speed (bottom graph), which originate at the solar surface and are imparted with a spiral appearance due to the Sun's roughly 27-day rotation.
