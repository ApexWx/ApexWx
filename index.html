NWS Caribou Cold Water Safety Message

The warm air temperatures this weekend in the lower 80s may cause  people underestimate the dangers of the cold water temperatures which are currently only in the mid 50s on the lakes  and in the mid 60s on the rivers.

The cold water temperatures can quickly cause hypothermia to anyone immersed in the water. According to the Maine Warden Service, when the water temperature is below 60 degrees, the average submerged person could lose dexterity within minutes and be unable to accomplish simple tasks. Anyone on small boats, canoes or kayaks should plan accordingly if recreating this weekend and use extreme caution to avoid this threat. Paddle smart from the start and always wear your life jacket!

Strong winds are also expected this weekend with gusts up to 24 mph from the southwest. If boating on large bodies of water, these strong winds can increase the likelihood of overturning your boat, canoe or kayak due to increased wave heights.

Short-Term Outlook: Saturday -  Monday

Low pressure tracking by to the north of the region Saturday will draw a cold front towards the region. This will bring a chance for showers and afternoon thunderstorms to the region. Isolated strong to severe storms are possible during the afternoon period thanks to favorable atmospheric dynamics aloft. The greatest potential for strong/severe storms is towards the Bangor and interior Downeast area; however, the SJV will also see the potential for some stronger storms along and ahead of the cold front.

Storms will diminish this evening as the front moves through and daytime heating wanes. Showers are expected to continue overnight with areas of patchy fog forming. Precipitation is expected to end early Sunday morning.

High pressure builds in Sunday morning with mostly sunny skies and increasingly warm conditions for the start of the new week. Partly cloudy skies Sunday night give way to mostly sunny skies Monday.

Monday will be hot and muggy with temperatures near or possibly breaking the current record high for June 7 (91° in 1976). While there is a slight chance for a pop shower or storm, the day is expected to be hot and dry otherwise.

Medium-Range Outlook: Tuesday - Friday

High pressure will continue to bring hot and humid temperatures to Fort Kent and vicinity Tuesday with temperatures potentially exceeding the record high of 84° set June 8, 1941. An approaching cold front will bring a slight chance for showers and isolated afternoon thunderstorms; however, there is not a great deal of moisture and instability anticipated from the front based on current computer models.

The front will bring a chance for showers Wednesday with cooler, less humid conditions for the late week period as Canadian high pressure builds down from the northwest. Mostly sunny to partly cloudy skies are expected Thursday and Friday.
