NWS Caribou Hazardous Weather Outlook
Strong southerly wind gusts up to 35 to 40 mph are expected this afternoon. Conditions will be hazardous to small craft on lakes and rivers.

Short-Term Outlook: Monday - Wednesday

High pressure will slide east of the region today while a strengthening low-pressure area near James Bay in Canada will create a strong pressure gradient leading to gusty south winds across the Valley today. Skies will be sunny this morning across Fort Kent and vicinity with clouds increasing this afternoon as a warm front associated with the low-pressure area lifts across the SJV. Some isolated showers are possible; however, there is not a great deal of moisture associated with this system at present. Showers continue overnight into Tuesday.

Partly sunny to partly cloudy skies are expected Tuesday with a slight chance for showers, mainly in the afternoon/evening hours as a cold front slowly moves across the region. The front will stall out across the state with showers expected tomorrow night into Wednesday. Enough instability will be present to allow for a slight chance for thunderstorms Wednesday afternoon as the front lifts back northwards as a warm front. A chance for showers and a slight chance for thunderstorms continues Wednesday night into Thursday across the SJV.

Paulette
Forecast Track

At 1100 AM AST / 11 AM EDT (1500 UTC) Monday, the center of Tropical Storm Paulette was located near latitude 17.2 North, longitude 42.2 West. Paulette is moving toward the west-northwest near 3 mph (6 km/h). A general  west-northwestward to northwestward motion at a slightly faster  speed is expected during the next several days.

Maximum sustained winds have increased to near 40 mph (65 km/h) with higher gusts. Modest additional strengthening is expected during the next couple of days. Tropical-storm-force winds extend outward up to 70 miles (110 km) from the center. The estimated minimum central pressure is 1005 mb (29.68 inches).

Tropical Depression Eighteen
Forecast Track

At 200 PM CVT / 11 AM EDT (1500 UTC) Monday, the center of Tropical Depression Eighteen was located near latitude 15.7 North, longitude 21.2 West. The depression is moving toward the west-northwest near 10 mph (17 km/h) and this general motion is expected over the next few days.  On the  forecast track, the center of the cyclone is forecast to pass near or over the Cabo Verde Islands later today and tonight.

Maximum sustained winds are near 35 mph (55 km/h) with higher gusts. Gradual strengthening is forecast during the next few days, and the depression is forecast to become a tropical storm later today. The estimated minimum central pressure is 1004 mb (29.65 inches).

NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center Forecast Discussion

Today's Sun
This near real-time image of the earth-facing side of the Sun is close to the Sun's actual appearance. Sunspots appear as dark splotches on the solar disk. They are associated with increased solar activity, including solar flares and coronal mass ejections [CMEs]. CMEs can trigger aurora displays on earth along with other geomagnetic phenomena. The sunspot images update every hour (click for larger image and see timestamp at lower left). Visit NASA's Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO) for more images and information.



 Coronal Holes
The dark areas - called coronal holes - are places where very little radiation is emitted, yet are the main source of solar wind particles. View 48-hour video.


Filaments and prominences

This channel is especially good at showing areas where cooler dense plumes of plasma (filaments and prominences) are located above the visible surface of the Sun. View 48-hour video.

Sunspot Regions Chart
The Solar Synoptic Map has been created daily since 1972 by forecasters at the NOAA SWPC. Map phenomena include active regions (including flare and proton event probabilities for each region), coronal holes, neutral lines (boundary between magnetic polarities), plages, filaments and prominences. Click image for more information (on website click "Details" for explanation of chart markings).


LASCO C2
Closer view of the corona (white circle indicates solar disk location). See image caption below for more information.


LASCO C3
LASCO (Large Angle Spectrometric Coronagraph) artificially blocks the solar disk (the shadow crossing from the lower left corner to the center of the image) to monitor the Sun's corona. Coronal streamers, periodic CMEs, bright background stars, planets (e.g., Venus), and occasionally a comet are visible in the image. The white circle in center indicates solar disk location.

Space Weather Overview

WSA-Enlil Solar Wind Prediction
WSA-Enlil is a large-scale, physics-based prediction model of the heliosphere, used by the Space Weather Forecast Office to provide 1-4 day advance warning of solar wind structures and Earth-directed coronal mass ejections (CMEs) that cause geomagnetic storms. The solar wind is a fast-moving stream of charged particles emanating from the Sun and moving outwards towards the Earth and planets. The solar wind contains significant variations in density (top graph) and speed (bottom graph), which originate at the solar surface and are imparted with a spiral appearance due to the Sun's roughly 27-day rotation.
