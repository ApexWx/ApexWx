  Short-Term Outlook: Sunday - Tuesday

Mostly cloudy skies are expected today with some lingering showers across the Valley as an upper trough slides across the region. The trough will advance to the coast later today with skies becoming partly cloudy to mostly clear as high-pressure in far northwest Canada spreads into the region tonight through Tuesday. Northwest winds will be somewhat breezy Monday as the ridge of high-pressure moves into the area. Mostly clear skies Monday and Monday night will see temperatures dip into the low 40s/upper 30s across northern Maine. Increased cloudiness is expected Tuesday afternoon and evening along with a slight chance for showers as another weak disturbance moves through the SJV.

Medium-Range: Wednesday - Saturday

A warm front crosses the region Wednesday with some increased cloudiness and a slight chance for showers. Southerly flow behind the high-pressure ridge moving east will bring warmer, more humid air into the Valley Wednesday through Friday. A cold front moves through Thursday with a chance for mainly afternoon/evening showers. This front slows along the coast keeping a chance for showers across the region Friday. A stronger cold front approaches Saturday with increased chances for showers and possibly some afternoon thunderstorms.

Cristobal (forecast track)
At 700 AM CDT (1200 UTC), the center of Tropical Storm Cristobal was located by an Air Force Reserve reconnaissance aircraft near latitude 28.2 North, longitude 89.9 West. Cristobal is moving toward the north near 12 mph (19 km/h) and this general motion is expected to continue today, followed by a gradual turn toward the north-northwest late today or tonight.  

On the forecast track, the center of Cristobal will approach the northern Gulf of Mexico coast this afternoon, then move inland across Louisiana late today through Monday morning, and northward across Arkansas and Missouri Monday afternoon into Tuesday. Data from NOAA Doppler weather radars indicate that maximum sustained winds remain near 50 mph (85 km/h) with higher gusts. Little change in strength is forecast before landfall, and weakening will begin once Cristobal moves inland.

Tropical-storm-force winds extend outward up to 205 miles (335 km) mainly to the east of the center.  A National Ocean Service station at the Southwest Pass of the Mississippi River recently reported a sustained wind of 43 mph (69 km/h) and a gust to 51 mph (82 km/h) at an elevation of 78 feet (24 m). The minimum central pressure recently measured by the reconnaissance aircraft was 994 mb (29.35 inches).

See "Key Messages" regarding Cristobal from the Nationa Hurricane Center: https://www.nhc.noaa.gov/refresh/graphics_at3+shtml/115216.shtml?key_messages#contents

NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center Forecast Discussion

Today's Sun
This near real-time image of the earth-facing side of the Sun is close to the Sun's actual appearance. Sunspots appear as dark splotches on the solar disk. They are associated with increased solar activity, including solar flares and coronal mass ejections [CMEs]. CMEs can trigger aurora displays on earth along with other geomagnetic phenomena. The sunspot images update every hour (click for larger image and see timestamp at lower left). Visit NASA's Solar Dynamics Observatory (SDO) for more images and information.



 Coronal Holes
The dark areas - called coronal holes - are places where very little radiation is emitted, yet are the main source of solar wind particles. View 48-hour video.


Filaments and prominences

This channel is especially good at showing areas where cooler dense plumes of plasma (filaments and prominences) are located above the visible surface of the Sun. View 48-hour video.

Sunspot Regions Chart
The Solar Synoptic Map has been created daily since 1972 by forecasters at the NOAA SWPC. Map phenomena include active regions (including flare and proton event probabilities for each region), coronal holes, neutral lines (boundary between magnetic polarities), plages, filaments and prominences. Click image for more information (on website click "Details" for explanation of chart markings).



LASCO C2
Closer view of the corona (white circle indicates solar disk location). See image caption below for more information.



LASCO C3
LASCO (Large Angle Spectrometric Coronagraph) artificially blocks the solar disk (the shadow crossing from the lower left corner to the center of the image) to monitor the Sun's corona. Coronal streamers, periodic CMEs, bright background stars, planets (e.g., Venus), and occasionally a comet are visible in the image. The white circle in center indicates solar disk location.

Space Weather Overview
These graphs track solar activity and the potential for solar flares that may produce aurora activity. Visit NOAA Space Weather Scales for an explanation of the intensity scales used in the graphs above.



WSA-Enlil Solar Wind Prediction
WSA-Enlil is a large-scale, physics-based prediction model of the heliosphere, used by the Space Weather Forecast Office to provide 1-4 day advance warning of solar wind structures and Earth-directed coronal mass ejections (CMEs) that cause geomagnetic storms. The solar wind is a fast-moving stream of charged particles emanating from the Sun and moving outwards towards the Earth and planets. The solar wind contains significant variations in density (top graph) and speed (bottom graph), which originate at the solar surface and are imparted with a spiral appearance due to the Sun's roughly 27-day rotation.
