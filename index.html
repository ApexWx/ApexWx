3-Day Outlook: Friday - Sunday

An occluded front moved through this morning and will continue to track east into the Maritimes today. High pressure will bring decreasing clouds and mostly sunny skies across the SJV by this afternoon. Light southwest winds and temperatures in the middle 50s will make for a pleasant day.

A cold front trailing from Canadian low pressure will move towards the Valley tonight with increasing clouds and a chance for showers after midnight. Showers continue into Saturday morning as the cold front crosses the region. Plenty of clouds and light precipitation will keep temperatures about 10 degrees cooler Saturday.

Low pressure tracking along the coast Saturday night will bring a chance for snow showers as colder air is drawn into the Valley. Snow showers continue into Sunday morning before changing to rain as the low tracks into the Maritimes and temperatures warm as the morning progresses.

Rain continues into Sunday afternoon as a large upper-level low crosses the region then tapers to showers as the afternoon progresses. Gusty northwest winds will push wind chill values into the 30s making for a rather chilly day in Fort Kent and vicinity. Mostly cloudy skies are expected Sunday night with rain showers mixing with snow showers overnight.

The upper-level low will move eastwards Saturday with a surface low drawing a cold front across the region. Rain is likely Saturday morning with a chance for rain through the afternoon and evening. Behind the cold front, colder air will be drawn into the region with a rain/snow mix overnight changing to all snow by Sunday morning.

NWS Caribou Ice Movement / Flood Outlook
The 9 mile ice jam that was located between Dickey and St. Francis is on the move and appears to be approaching Ft. Kent as of 4 am this morning. Latest information we have is that it is flowing freely and the river is high but have not received an update in the past few hours. There is another small jam that between Lille and Grand Isle mainly impacting low lying farm fields near the river banks.

The latest snow survey data from Monday and Tuesday indicates snow water equivalent of 5 to 9 inches and snow depth of 18 to 29 inches from Winterville north through Allagash, indicating that there is still a significant amount of snow left to melt across the Saint John and Allagash River basins. Further south along Route 11, snow water equivalents were less than 5 inches with snow depths less than 15 inches.

High temperatures will reach upper 40s to mid 50s for the next few days. Continued river level rises are likely and ice jams remain possible over the Saint John River into this weekend. Precipitation will mainly fall in the form of rain through the weekend and be generally less than 0.5 inch total over the St. John Valley. Any additional rainfall will be falling into "ripe" snowpack so the potential for accelerated snowmelt is possible.

For information on the region's Spring Flood Outlook, see https://forecast.weather.gov/product.php?site=car&issuedby=CAR&product=ESF.
